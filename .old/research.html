Research Interests

My group is primarily interested is in the development and validation of tools for effective and efficient delivery of neurorehabilitation (in particular for stroke). We focus on both tools for movement therapy, and methods for the quantitative assessment of sensorimotor behaviour. Effective tools and methods can only result from a good understanding of the science behind healthy and stroke affected motor control and learning. Thus, we also carry out human motor control/learning studies with relevance to neurorehabilitation.

Tools for neurorehabilitation therapy

System for Independent Task-oriented Assessment and Rehabilitation (SITAR)

System for Independent Task-oriented Assessment and Rehabilitation is an instrumented multi-modal workstation for patients to training and assessment upper-extremity (UE) tasks related to activities of daily living. SITAR was originally developed by the Human Robotics Group, Imperial College London. We have recently completed a usability study with the SITAR evaluating it on patients with arm impairments. Our current work is focused on the use of SITAR for upper-extremity assessment, and its effectiveness for improving UE impairments and function.

Virtual reality for vestibular rehabilitation

This projects aims to develop and test the usability of a Google cardboard based head mounted display for vestibular rehabilitation of patients. This is being pursued as a MS thesis by Aravind, in collaboration with ENT @ CMC Vellore.

An intelligent system for automatic dropfoot correction with FES

The aim of this project is to develop an intelligent system for drop foot correction that can be taught by the user to routinely improve its gait detection performance during steady state walking. The current study will develop the system and carry out a pilot study to evaluate its performance. This project is being pursued as a MS thesis by Naveen. This project is being led by Dr Suresh Devasahayam from Bioengineering, and in collaboration with PMR @ CMC Vellore.

Quantitative sensorimotor assessment

SPectral ARC length measure of movement smoothness

We have recently developed a general method for quantifying the smoothness of different types of movement. This generalization is an extension of the novel method that we had proposed in 2012 to quantify smoothness. This new general method is suitable for use in rhythmic movements, and on movement related data other than movement kinematics. The details of the analysis to study SPARC's properties can be found here.

[1] Balasubramanian, Sivakumar, et al. "On the analysis of movement smoothness." Journal of neuroengineering and rehabilitation 12.1 (2015): 1. [pdf]
[2] Balasubramanian, Sivakumar, Alejandro Melendez-Calderon, and Etienne Burdet. "A robust and sensitive metric for quantifying movement smoothness." Biomedical Engineering, IEEE Transactions on 59.8 (2012): 2126-2136.

Others

Wound volume quantification of pressure ulcers

To develop and validate a method for quantifying wound volume of pressure uclers using a Microsoft Kinect 2.0 (RGBD) camera. The current focus is on the algorithm development for volume quantification of objects of known geometry. This validation will be followed by collection of data from patients with pressure ulcers, and the estimation of wound volume. This work is being carried out in collaboration with Dr Blessed from Pharmacology @ CMC Vellore. This project is also an ongoing MS thesis, pursued by Ms Shilpa.
